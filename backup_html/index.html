The belief

Social media forgot what social means.

You don't open Instagram to connect. You open it to consume. The feed is a machine designed to hold your attention, not to introduce you to your people. Every platform optimised for reach and got performance. For engagement and got addiction.

Meanwhile, the truest parts of you — the film that broke something open, the album you only play when you need to feel something real, the book you've been trying to give everyone you love — have nowhere to live. They sit in private notes apps and dog-eared pages and conversations that end too soon.

We think the self you are in those private moments is the most interesting one. And we think that self is the best basis for finding people who actually matter to you.

What it is

Four things that work together.

01
Your taste map
Add the films, music, books, articles, threads — anything that has genuinely meant something to you. Write what it did to you, not what you think of it. The map builds itself from your honesty.
Living portrait
02
Your collections
Cabinet-of-curiosities style boards. A folder for what makes loneliness feel smaller. A chapter of your life. Everything that reminds you of someone. Links, images, writing, videos — held together by feeling.
Private archive
03
What you want to do
Write the things you want to do that feel too big or too odd to say most places. Build a startup. Go on a solo trip. Find someone to explore this city with properly. These shape who we find for you.
Intentional future
04
The people nearby
When your map has enough of you in it, we find the people in your city whose inner world rhymes with yours. Not by age or profession — by what moves them, and what they want. Then we suggest something real you could do together.

A story

Meet Arjun. 26, Pune.

I.

The first night.

He opens the app and it asks him one question. Not "add your favourite movies." Just — what's something you consumed recently that stayed with you longer than you expected?

"Paterson. The Jim Jarmusch film. It made me feel like ordinary life was secretly enormous, and that scared me because I wasn't sure I was living it that way."

II.

The first week.

He comes back not because of a notification — because he thought of something he wanted to add. The taste map is forming. He notices almost everything he loves has something to do with quiet, and time, and the dignity of small things. The map showed him a pattern in himself.

III.

The resonance.

The app tells him — someone nearby resonates with your map. Not a face and a bio. Just the shape of what they share. Same film. Same particular melancholy in music. Both wrote something about wanting to build something.

"Her name is Nadia. Her map has things he doesn't recognise and wants to."

IV.

The meeting.

A restored Italian film at a small theatre in Koregaon Park. Afterwards, chai. Two hours. About the film, about the city, about what they're both quietly trying to build. At no point does it feel like a first meeting. It feels like a conversation that was already in progress and they just found where it had been waiting.
